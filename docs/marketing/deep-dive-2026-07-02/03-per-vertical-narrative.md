# Per-vertical narrative — ICP, pains, before/with, objections, proof to earn

**How to use this:** this is the story spine for every asset in a vertical — landing copy, ads, outreach, content. Pains are written in the owner's words, not ours (`feedback_customer_vocab_not_engineer`: customers say "chasing paperwork," not "workflow orchestration"). The before/with contrast is the ad. The objections are real and get answered, not dodged. The proof points are **to be earned in the next 90 days, never fabricated** — until earned, the surface stays silent on them.

**Universal rules riding every narrative:**
- The control line appears every time: *the fleet drafts and proposes; you approve and send.*
- Cadence language, not live-magic language (degraded mode is today's reality).
- ROI figures only as cited in `lib/verticals/<slug>/content.ts`; trial terms verbatim from `lib/billing/facts.ts` (7-day trial, card at signup; 14-day for CPA and law; 14-day money-back).

## 1. Real estate (beachhead)

**The ICP owner:** a broker-owner or team lead running 2–15 agents in a metro market, personally producing while also running the business. Their phone is the office. They already pay for a CRM and a transaction platform, and they still spend nights on the work between those tools: answering inbound leads fast enough, chasing commission invoices, writing the recap nobody else will write. They measure everything in response time and closings.

**Top 3 pains, their words:**
1. "If I don't answer a lead in five minutes, they've already texted three other agents."
2. "I'm chasing my own commission checks. Nobody pays me on time unless I nag."
3. "I get to my desk at 9pm and there are forty emails that all need two sentences from me."

**Before / with:**
- *Before:* leads sit until you surface; invoice follow-ups happen when you remember; the transaction recap never gets written; compliance is a thing you hope about.
- *With agentplain:* every inbound lead gets a drafted, qualifying first-touch waiting in your queue; commission-invoice chases draft themselves against QuickBooks on a cadence; an overnight transaction summary is on your desk in the morning; every draft passed a fair-housing review before you ever saw it. You still hit send on all of it.

**3 objections and the honest answers:**
1. *"I already have Follow Up Boss / BoldTrail."* Good — keep it. They organize the pipeline; we draft the work around it. Different job.
2. *"AI will say something that gets me sued."* Nothing sends without you. Drafts are reviewed against the fair-housing corpus before you see them, and the first-offense HUD penalty ($26,262) is exactly why we built it draft-first.
3. *"I don't have time to set up another tool."* You don't set it up. We install, configure, and run a monthly review — that's the service.

**Proof to earn (next 90 days):**
1. Three named, permissioned design-partner quotes from working brokers (the founder-outreach motion's primary output).
2. One measured saved-time figure from a real partner workspace, pulled from the product's own ledger — which requires closing the audit-dept-9 gap first (4 of 7 calibrated actions write no saved-time minutes today).
3. One golden-hour photograph of a real partner in their market (photography briefs exist at `docs/brand/photography-briefs-2026-06-22.md`; zero produced photos today).

## 2. CPA / accounting firms

**The ICP owner:** a firm owner or partner at a 1–10 person practice, CPA or EA, whose year has two speeds: drowning (January–April) and catching up (the rest). They bill for judgment but spend hours on document chases and "just checking in" emails. They are professionally allergic to anything that files, sends, or signs on its own, and they've been burned by software that promised to save the busy season and didn't.

**Top 3 pains, their words:**
1. "Half of tax season is chasing clients for documents they swore they sent."
2. "Clients want a status update every week, and writing them eats my afternoons."
3. "I can't take on more clients without hiring, and I can't find anyone to hire."

**Before / with:**
- *Before:* the missing-doc list lives in your head; status updates go out late or not at all; AR aging is a report you dread opening; every new client means an onboarding letter written from scratch at 11pm.
- *With agentplain:* document chases draft themselves per client, in your voice, on a schedule; weekly status updates land in your queue ready to approve; QuickBooks AR gets summarized with follow-ups pre-drafted; onboarding letters are waiting before the engagement letter is signed. A credentialed person — you — approves every one.

**3 objections and the honest answers:**
1. *"My client data can't sit on some vendor's server."* It doesn't sit. The fleet reads your connected tools where the data lives; what persists is working memory about your practice, kept for the life of your account. Your files stay in your systems.
2. *"Anything client-facing has my license behind it."* Correct, and the architecture agrees: nothing auto-sends, ever. Preparer-penalty exposure is why the approval queue exists.
3. *"Software always promises to fix tax season."* Which is why the trial is 14 days for CPA firms, card at signup, with a 14-day money-back guarantee. Judge it on what lands in your queue in week one.

**Proof to earn (next 90 days):**
1. One CPA design partner recruited through the RE-partner referral network or direct outreach (packet exists in `docs/marketing/design-partner-outreach/`).
2. A documented month-end-close run on a real firm's QuickBooks (the wired integration), written up with the partner's permission.
3. A busy-season readiness story we can stand behind *before* January — even "here's exactly what it drafted in a normal week" with real screenshots beats a claim.

## 3. Law firms

**The ICP owner:** a managing partner at a 1–5 attorney firm (family, estate, PI, or general practice) who bills six figures of time a year and still writes their own follow-up emails. They are the most risk-literate buyer we have: confidentiality (Rule 1.6) and advertising rules (Rule 7.1) are reflexes, not concerns. They will read the privacy page. They distrust anything that sounds automated and anything that sounds too good.

**Top 3 pains, their words:**
1. "Clients call because they haven't heard from us, not because anything went wrong."
2. "Intake falls through the cracks. Someone calls, we're in court, they hire whoever answered."
3. "My associates spend billable hours on emails a paralegal could send, and I can't hire a paralegal."

**Before / with:**
- *Before:* status letters slip weeks; intake follow-ups depend on whoever checked the inbox; the conflict check is a memory exercise; routine correspondence eats associate hours the firm can't bill.
- *With agentplain:* client status updates draft on a cadence with the matter context read in; every intake gets a drafted follow-up and a deterministic adverse-party screen, with the legal conclusion left as a merge field for the attorney; document-request chases write themselves. An attorney approves every client-facing word.

**3 objections and the honest answers:**
1. *"Rule 1.6. Full stop."* The data posture is pass-through: your files are read in your systems, not copied into ours; what we keep is working memory about your practice, for the life of your account, and the privacy page names every subprocessor. Bring your ethics counsel's questions — the answers are written down.
2. *"AI drafts legal conclusions? No."* It doesn't. The conflict screen is deterministic and the conclusion field is left blank for you. The division of labor is the design, not a disclaimer.
3. *"We're too small to need this."* You're exactly the size that needs it: the pain is that the partners *are* the admin staff. Regular tier is $199/seat sliding to $99; the 14-day law-firm trial with a 14-day money-back guarantee means the risk is one hour of setup conversation.

**Proof to earn (next 90 days):**
1. One law-firm design partner with an ethics-counsel-reviewed onboarding (their counsel, on record as having reviewed — the review itself is the proof asset).
2. The counsel packet for our own legal pages closed (audit dept 1, finding 6): lawyer buyers read those pages first, and "counsel-reviewed" must be true of us before we imply diligence to them.
3. One permissioned workflow story: a week of intake follow-ups and status drafts, described concretely with the firm's sign-off.

## 4. Property management

**The ICP owner:** an independent property manager or small PM company owner, 50–400 doors, running on AppFolio or Buildium with two coordinators. Their day is tenant emails, owner anxiety, and rent chasing. Owners judge them monthly; tenants judge them hourly. They fear fair-housing complaints and losing doors to bigger firms with call centers.

**Top 3 pains, their words:**
1. "Late rent means writing the same firm-but-polite email fifty times a month."
2. "Owners want to feel informed. If I'm quiet, they think I'm hiding something."
3. "Every tenant email is a chance to get a fair-housing complaint if someone words it wrong."

**Before / with:**
- *Before:* delinquency follow-ups happen in batches when someone has an hour; owner updates are quarterly apologies; tenant replies get written fast and unreviewed; the ledger is current but the communication around it is weeks behind.
- *With agentplain:* late-rent notices draft on the delinquency cadence, firm and human; monthly owner notes draft themselves from what actually happened; tenant-facing drafts get a compliance review pass before you approve; the correspondence catches up to the ledger. You approve and send, every time.

**3 objections and the honest answers:**
1. *"AppFolio already has automation."* It automates inside its own walls, and it sends. We draft across your inbox and books, and nothing leaves without you. Keep AppFolio; we're the correspondence layer.
2. *"Fair housing scares me more than the admin does."* Us too — that's why drafts are reviewed against your rules before you see them, and a human approves everything. (In-market copy for PM says "reviewed against your rules"; the live fair-housing scanner covers real estate today, and we don't claim otherwise.)
3. *"Per-door pricing already eats my margin."* We don't price per door. Per seat: the manager, not the portfolio. The cited math is ~$36,000/yr back in PM-hours and delinquency reclamation against $199/seat.

**Proof to earn (next 90 days):**
1. One PM design partner (likely referral-adjacent to the RE partners — brokers know property managers).
2. A permissioned before/after of one delinquency cycle: what drafted, what the manager edited, what went out.
3. Extension of the live compliance scanner to PM tenant comms *or* honest copy that keeps the distinction — the proof is either the feature or the discipline, and both are fine; claiming the feature without shipping it is not.

## 5. General (all other local businesses)

**The ICP owner:** a local business owner — shop, studio, trades-adjacent office, small agency — with 1–10 people, no ops manager, and an inbox that is the real to-do list. They've tried a chat AI and found it useful the way a search engine is useful: only when they remember to ask it something. They don't want to become a prompt person. They want the admin to be *handled*.

**Top 3 pains, their words:**
1. "I answer the same ten emails every week and it still takes me all of Sunday."
2. "I tried the AI chat things. It's homework. I don't have time for homework."
3. "I can't afford an assistant, and training one takes longer than doing the work."

**Before / with:**
- *Before:* the inbox runs the day; follow-ups happen when guilt strikes; the AI subscription goes unused because nobody's driving it; hiring is the fantasy solution that never pencils.
- *With agentplain:* the fleet reads what's already in your email, calendar, and books, and drafts the recurring work without being asked; drafts pile up in a queue you clear with coffee; it remembers your business, so week ten is better than week one. You stay the only one who hits send.

**3 objections and the honest answers:**
1. *"How is this different from the chat AI I already pay for?"* A chat window answers when you ask. This works your cadence whether you ask or not, on your real inbox and books, and everything waits for your approval. It's a service that runs, not a tool you drive.
2. *"My business is too weird for a template."* There's no template. The fleet learns your business from your own tools and its memory of you persists for the life of the account. And ten verticals already have dedicated depth — if yours is one, start there.
3. *"What's the catch on the trial?"* Card at signup, 7 days, cancel anytime, 14-day money-back after that. The catch is you'll have to read some drafts.

**Proof to earn (next 90 days):**
1. The general on-ramp conversion reading itself: how many non-vertical signups activate (measurement plan, `06-measurement.md`) — proof for *us* about whether general deserves ad spend.
2. One permissioned story from any general-vertical trial user, even unpaid: what the queue looked like in week one.
3. Our own dogfooding story told concretely and publicly: the brokerage fleet, what it drafts weekly, with real (internal) screenshots — the one production story that is already true.
