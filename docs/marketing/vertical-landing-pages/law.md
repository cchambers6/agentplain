## 2. The pain

You did not go to law school to chase documents.

- **Intake is a tax on every new matter.** Run the conflict search by hand, merge the engagement letter from a template, hope nothing slipped through. Day one of every matter starts with an hour you cannot bill.
- **The conflict-check anxiety never goes away.** One missed prior-matter adversity is a Model Rule 1.7 problem with your name on it. The search is only as good as the time you had that afternoon.
- **Clients only hear from you when something is wrong.** Status updates are reactive — the client calls you, not the other way around. Every call is a billable hour spent on a thing a draft could have handled.
- **Outbound is a privilege risk in every message.** One privileged detail sent to the wrong recipient is a confidentiality breach under Model Rule 1.6 — and a malpractice exposure no hours-saved number can offset.

## 3. What Plaino does for law firms

Plaino is the named partner across your workspace. The fleet reads your inbox, watches every client communication, and drafts the routine work so your attorneys spend their hours on judgment.

**Working from your email on day one — across every law workspace:**

- **Inbox triage.** Every message tagged by priority; gentle acknowledgements drafted for client-active and vendor-pending threads. Urgent and needs-decision mail always goes straight to you.
- **Follow-up chasing.** Stale outbound threads surfaced and a calm nudge drafted — oldest first, never piling up the draft folder.
- **First-touch support drafts.** Inbound client questions get a grounded first draft for an attorney to approve.
- **Weekly activity pulse.** One calm Monday read of what the fleet did, where it was underused, and one thing to lean into next.
- **Compliance watch.** Every morning the fleet runs the prior day's drafts through your vertical's sentinel corpus and a PII pattern set, then surfaces a digest of anything to review before approving. It advises; it never blocks or edits on its own.
- **Chief-of-staff scheduling.** Meetings, reply drafts, and to-dos proposed against the attorney's calendar and inbox. Connect Google or Microsoft 365 to activate the calendar side.

**The intake conflict screen — the law-specific workflow.**

The intake conflict screen runs a deterministic conflict screen on a prospective-client intake. It compares the prospect and opposing parties against your firm ledger, classifies each hit as direct, adverse, or former-adverse, and drafts a formal internal notice to the responsible attorney. The legal conclusion is a merge field — the fleet never asserts it. The responsible attorney fills it in and decides.

It is grounded in the ABA Model Rules of Professional Conduct 1.7 and 1.18 as the conflict-screening framework — not as legal advice.

> **Setting up:** Your practice-management system (Clio, MyCase, PracticePanther) connects as it comes online. Today the screen accepts your ledger and screens against email-sourced intakes. The same workflow binds to the practice-management system once that connection lands — no change to how you work.

**The value loop — a real example.**

Civil litigation matter. Discovery production due Friday: 4,200 documents to review and categorize.

- **Before:** Three associates split the review. Two days of doc-by-doc privilege and responsiveness coding. The partner spot-checks the borderline calls; a paralegal builds the privilege log. About 60 billable hours, of which maybe 8 are real judgment.
- **With the fleet:** First-pass responsiveness and privilege coding runs against the matter's index. 312 borderline calls flagged. Privilege-log entries drafted, every borderline call citing the rule, cross-referenced against the firm's prior-matter privilege index.
- **The outcome:** Associates review the 312 borderline calls; the partner spot-checks 30. Production ships Friday morning. The 60 hours becomes 14 — the 14 spent on actual judgment, not page-by-page coding.
