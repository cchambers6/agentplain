# Georgia Home Study Law — Compliance Spec for the Registrar Agent

**Jurisdiction:** Georgia (statewide; no county/district variation in home study requirements since 2013)
**Controlling statute:** O.C.G.A. § 20-2-690(c) — "Educational entities; requirements for private schools and home study programs; learning pod protection"
**Companion statute:** O.C.G.A. § 20-2-690.1 — compulsory attendance (ages 6–16); home study is one of three ways to satisfy it
**Verified:** 2026-07-10, against FindLaw's Georgia Code (current through March 28, 2024), ga.elaws.us, the Georgia DOE Declaration of Intent portal, and HSLDA's Georgia compliance guide. All statutory quotes below are verbatim.

> **Citation correction (important):** The home study requirements live in **§ 20-2-690(c)**, not § 20-2-690.1. Section 20-2-690.1 is the compulsory-attendance statute (mandatory education ages 6–16, penalties, withdrawal at 16). Any product copy or agent prompt citing "§ 20-2-690.1" for home study requirements is citing the wrong section. The GaDOE's own DOI form cites § 20-2-690.

## 1. The statute

Home study programs are authorized and regulated by **O.C.G.A. § 20-2-690(c)**, which contains exactly **eight numbered paragraphs**. Subsections (a)–(b) of the same section govern private schools; subsection (d) makes violation a misdemeanor punishable by a fine not exceeding $100. A "learning pod protection" provision was added to the section in 2021 (reflected in the section's current title).

**O.C.G.A. § 20-2-690.1** (the compulsory-attendance statute) is what makes home study legally meaningful: it requires every parent of a child aged 6–16 to enroll the child in a public school, private school, or home study program, with penalties ($25–$100 fines, up to 30 days imprisonment) for violations. A compliant § 20-2-690(c) program is the parent's legal defense to a truancy charge.

## 2. Declaration of Intent — § 20-2-690(c)(1)–(2)

Statutory text, (c)(1):

> "The parent, parents, or guardian must submit within 30 days after the establishment of a home study program and by September 1 annually thereafter a declaration of intent to utilize a home study program to the Department of Education, which shall provide for written or electronic submittal of such declaration of intent."

Statutory text, (c)(2):

> "The declaration shall include a list of the names and ages of the students who are enrolled in the home study program, the address where the home study program is located, the local school system in which the home study program is located, and a statement of the 12 month period that is to be considered the school year for that home study program."

Operational facts (verified against the GaDOE DOI portal, apps.gadoe.org):

- **Who files:** the parent, parents, or guardian. Filing is with the **Georgia Department of Education** (state level), not the local district — this changed in 2013 (HB 283); before that, filing was with the local superintendent.
- **How:** GaDOE provides an online web application (desktop/laptop; email-keyed; three steps: email → review → submit). Written submission (mail/fax) also satisfies the statute's "written or electronic" language, but GaDOE strongly encourages online filing because it issues a **confirmation number** — the parent's proof of filing. The portal warns users to print/save the confirmed DOI before leaving the page.
- **When:** within **30 days** of establishing the program, and **by September 1 annually** thereafter.
- **Downstream:** GaDOE is required to make a copy of the DOI available to the local school system (per the portal's own statement). False statements on the DOI implicate O.C.G.A. § 16-10-71 (false swearing), which the portal cites.
- **Confidentiality:** (c)(2) also provides that "Enrollment records and reports shall not be used for any purpose except providing necessary enrollment information, except with the permission of the parent or guardian of a child, or pursuant to the subpoena of a court of competent jurisdiction."

**Registrar implications:** the declared 12-month school year in the DOI is the anchor for everything else — the 180-day count, the annual progress report, and the Sept-1 renewal reminder all key off it. Chiron must store the DOI filing date, the confirmation number, the declared year window, and fire a renewal reminder well before each September 1.

## 3. Instructional time — § 20-2-690(c)(5)

Verbatim:

> "The home study program must provide instruction each 12 months to home study students equivalent to 180 school days of education with each school day consisting of at least four and one-half school hours unless the child is physically unable to comply with the rule provided for in this paragraph."

Notes on the exact phrasing:

- The unit is "**equivalent to** 180 school days" per rolling 12-month program year — the statute measures days, not total hours, but defines a qualifying day as "at least four and one-half school hours."
- The only stated exception is a child "physically unable to comply."
- The statute does not prescribe which dates within the 12 months, or any minimum per-week cadence.

## 4. Required subjects — § 20-2-690(c)(4)

Verbatim:

> "The home study program shall provide a basic academic educational program which includes, but is not limited to, reading, language arts, mathematics, social studies, and science."

The list is exactly five subjects — **reading, language arts, mathematics, social studies, and science** — and is a floor, not a ceiling ("includes, but is not limited to"). Note that "reading" and "language arts" are listed as *separate* subjects; the annual progress report in (c)(8) must address "each of the subject areas specified in paragraph (4)," so Chiron's subject taxonomy and the annual report template must treat them as five distinct areas.

## 5. Instructor qualification — § 20-2-690(c)(3)

Verbatim:

> "Parents or guardians may teach only their own children in the home study program, provided the teaching parent or guardian possesses at least a high school diploma or a state approved high school equivalency (HSE) diploma, but the parents or guardians may employ a tutor who holds a high school diploma or a state approved high school equivalency (HSE) diploma to teach such children."

- Current statutory wording is "state approved high school equivalency (HSE) diploma" — the older "GED" wording was replaced (many secondary sources, including HSLDA, still say "GED"; GED is one brand of HSE). Product copy should say "high school diploma or state-approved high school equivalency (HSE) diploma."
- A parent may teach **only their own children**; a hired tutor with the same credential may teach them. There is no provision for teaching other families' children in a home study program (the 2021 learning-pod provision protects pods but does not alter this paragraph).

## 6. Annual progress assessment report — § 20-2-690(c)(8)

Verbatim:

> "The home study program instructor shall write an annual progress assessment report which shall include the instructor's individualized assessment of the student's academic progress in each of the subject areas specified in paragraph (4) of this subsection, and such progress reports shall be retained by the parent, parents, or guardian of children in the home study program for a period of at least three years."

Requirements extracted:

- **Author:** the instructor (usually the parent).
- **Frequency:** annual.
- **Content:** an "individualized assessment" of progress in **each of the five (c)(4) subjects**, per student.
- **Retention:** **at least three years**, by the parent/guardian. Confirmed — the 3-year figure is in the statute itself.
- **Submission:** none. The report is written and retained, never filed with any authority.

**Registrar implications:** Chiron should draft this report at the end of each declared school year from the accumulated subject-coverage logs, one section per statutory subject per student, and stamp each stored report with a retention-until date ≥ 3 years out (recommend retaining indefinitely; see § 9).

## 7. Standardized testing — § 20-2-690(c)(7)

Verbatim:

> "Students in home study programs shall be subject to an appropriate nationally standardized testing program administered in consultation with a person trained in the administration and interpretation of norm reference tests to evaluate their educational progress at least every three years beginning at the end of the third grade and records of such tests and scores shall be retained but shall not be required to be submitted to public educational authorities."

Requirements extracted:

- **Instrument:** "an appropriate nationally standardized testing program" — i.e., a national **norm-referenced** test (the statute says "norm reference tests" in describing the consultant). No specific test is named; parents choose (ITBS, Stanford, CAT, etc. all commonly used).
- **Administration:** "administered in consultation with a person trained in the administration and interpretation of norm reference tests." The parent may administer; the statute requires only *consultation* with a trained person, not administration *by* one.
- **Cadence:** "at least every three years beginning at the end of the third grade."
- **Retention/submission:** "records of such tests and scores **shall be retained** but **shall not be required to be submitted** to public educational authorities." No retention period is specified for test records (unlike the 3-year period for progress reports).
- **No pass threshold:** the statute sets no minimum score and attaches no consequence to results.

**Registrar implications:** per-student cadence tracker seeded at end of grade 3, next-due = last-test + 3 years (or grade-3 year-end for younger students); reminders ahead of the due window; secure storage of score reports with an indefinite retention default. Chiron must never offer to "file" or "submit" scores anywhere — the statute expressly negates any submission duty.

## 8. Attendance — what current law requires vs. what was repealed

**Current law requires no attendance records at all.** Verified against the full current text of § 20-2-690(c): none of the eight paragraphs requires keeping or submitting attendance records. The word "attendance" historically appeared in (c)(6)'s document-execution sentence (re: Department of Driver Services verification), and a **2015 amendment (effective July 1, 2015) substituted "enrollment" for "attendance"** even there.

**What was repealed:** the original 1984 home study law (SB 504) required parents to keep attendance records and **submit them monthly** to the local superintendent. **HB 283 (2013), the Comprehensive Education Bill, effective July 1, 2013**, removed the attendance-submission requirement and simultaneously moved DOI filing from the local school board to the Georgia DOE. Since then, no attendance form is submitted to anyone, monthly or otherwise.

**Caution on secondary sources:** CRHE and some homeschool-group pages state that parents must still "keep attendance records for three years." That claim does **not** match the current statutory text — the only 3-year retention duty in § 20-2-690(c) is for the (c)(8) progress reports. Chiron's product copy must not assert a statutory attendance-record duty that doesn't exist.

**Why Chiron still tracks days (best practice, not statutory duty):**

1. **(c)(5) is substantive** — the 180-day/4.5-hour requirement exists whether or not records are mandated. In a truancy inquiry under § 20-2-690.1, a day log is the parent's evidence of compliance.
2. **(c)(6) document execution** — parents must be able to "execute any document required by law... to evidence the enrollment of a child in a home study program, the student's full-time or part-time status, the student's grades, or any other required educational information," including DDS enrollment verification under § 40-5-22(a.1) (learner's permit/license), minor-employment documents under Title 39 Ch. 2, and public-assistance applications. Day and status records make those attestations defensible.

## 9. What Chiron generates — mapped to statutory basis

| Chiron artifact | Statutory basis | Framing to user |
|---|---|---|
| DOI renewal reminder (before Sept 1) + filing-record vault (date, confirmation number, declared year) | (c)(1)–(2) | Required |
| Instructional-day counter vs. 180-day target within the declared 12-month year; day = ≥ 4.5 hours | (c)(5) | Required standard; the *log itself* is best-practice evidence |
| Subject-coverage log across reading, language arts, math, social studies, science (five distinct areas) | (c)(4) | Required content of the program |
| Annual progress report draft — per student, one individualized section per (c)(4) subject — generated at declared year-end | (c)(8) | Required document; retain ≥ 3 years (Chiron default: indefinite) |
| Test-cadence tracker: first due end of grade 3, then every ≤ 3 years; reminder + score-report storage | (c)(7) | Required cadence; results retained, **never submitted** |
| Instructor-credential attestation (HS diploma / HSE) — one-time, per instructor/tutor | (c)(3) | Required qualification |
| On-demand enrollment/status/grade verification letters (DDS, employment of minors, public assistance) | (c)(6); § 40-5-22(a.1) | Parent's statutory authority; Chiron drafts, parent signs |
| Attendance/day-count export | none (repealed 2013) | Best practice / evidence — explicitly labeled non-statutory |

**Retention defaults:** progress reports ≥ 3 years (statutory minimum, (c)(8)); test records "retained" with no statutory period ((c)(7)) — default indefinite; everything else indefinite. Practical guidance widely given (and sensible for the product): keep all records through high school graduation + several years, since home study parents issue their own transcripts and diplomas and colleges/DDS/employers rely on parent-executed documents under (c)(6).

## Points of legal ambiguity (flagged for counsel / product copy review)

1. **Wrong-section risk:** the home study requirements are in § 20-2-690(c); § 20-2-690.1 is compulsory attendance. Task briefs and any existing copy citing 690.1 for home study requirements need correction.
2. **"Equivalent to 180 school days":** the statute doesn't define how equivalence is computed (e.g., whether two 2.25-hour sessions can aggregate to one day). Plain reading: a countable day requires ≥ 4.5 hours. Chiron should count only ≥ 4.5-hour days toward 180 and treat any aggregation as unsupported.
3. **"In consultation with a person trained in... norm reference tests"** ((c)(7)) is undefined — no credential, no documentation requirement. Chiron can capture the consultant's name as optional metadata but cannot state a verification standard the statute doesn't set.
4. **Test-record retention period:** (c)(7) says records "shall be retained" with no duration. Chiron defaults to indefinite; do not state "3 years" for test records (that period belongs only to progress reports).
5. **Attendance-records folklore:** multiple reputable-looking secondary sources (CRHE among them) assert a continuing duty to keep attendance records for three years. The current statutory text contains no such duty. Chiron tracks days as evidence, but copy must distinguish statutory requirements from best practice.
6. **HSE vs. GED wording:** statute says "state approved high school equivalency (HSE) diploma"; many secondary sources still say "GED." Use the statutory wording.
7. **Learning pods (2021):** the section title now includes "learning pod protection," but (c)(3) still limits parents to teaching "only their own children." Pod-related product features should get a dedicated read of the 2021 provision before making claims.
