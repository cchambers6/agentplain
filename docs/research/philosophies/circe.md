# CIRCE Institute (Andrew Kern) — Research Notes (Chiron)

CIRCE — "Center for Independent Research on Classical Education" (secondary acronym:
"Consulting and Integrated Resources for Classical Educators") — is the poetic/contemplative
wing of the classical renewal. Where Well-Trained Mind gives a stage-and-schedule system and
Memoria Press gives a Latin-centered curriculum, CIRCE supplies the *telos* and pedagogy:
education exists to cultivate wisdom and virtue.

## Organization and founder

- Founded by **Andrew Kern**, who began in 1996 as "Circe Ministries," a research and
  tutoring service formed while writing *Classical Education: The Movement Sweeping
  America* (with Gene Edward Veith) and teaching great-books classes to homeschooled high
  schoolers. It became a 501(c)(3), the CiRCE Institute, in 2002, the year it launched its
  annual conference (circeinstitute.org/about-us).
- Kern remains founder/president; CIRCE's offerings include the annual conference, an
  online academy, teacher apprenticeships, consulting for school start-up and governance,
  and *The Lost Tools of Writing* curriculum.
- Explicitly Christian: committed to "mere Christianity" as expressed in the historic
  creeds, especially the Nicene Creed (about-us page).
- Homepage tagline: "Cultivating Wisdom and Virtue" — the institute's four-word summary of
  classical education's purpose (this is this document's single direct quote,
  circeinstitute.org homepage).

## Core philosophy: wisdom, virtue, and the true/good/beautiful

From CIRCE's "What is Classical Education?" page, four controlling ideas:

1. **A high view of humanity** — the student bears the Divine Image; classical education
   cultivates wisdom and virtue in persons, and is therefore not reducible to vocational
   training.
2. **Logocentrism** — the world makes sense and that sense is knowable; education is a
   quest for the logos, the unifying principle of all knowledge and action. CIRCE's
   compressed definition: classical education is the logocentric quest for the ideals of
   wisdom and virtue.
3. **Responsibility for the Western tradition** — receive it, assess it, preserve it, hand
   it on.
4. **Teaching in light of the above** — emphasis on language (trivium), mathematics
   (quadrivium), and modes of teaching/governance/assessment that fit those goals; plus
   classical books and art, integrated curriculum, and idea-focused teaching.

The soul is treated as the "organ of truth perception"; teaching turns the soul toward
what is worth perceiving — the True, the Good, and the Beautiful — and strengthens it to
perceive them. Contemplation (of great texts, works, and ideas) is therefore the central
academic act, not information transfer. CIRCE's conferences are literally named
contemplations (e.g. "A Contemplation of Revolution," 2026 National Conference).

## Mimetic teaching

CIRCE works in two classical teaching modes — **Socratic** and **mimetic** — and treats
mimetic instruction as the workhorse of classical pedagogy (circeinstitute.org
/what-is-mimetic-teaching/, an excerpt from *The Lost Tools of Writing*):

- Humans learn and become virtuous **by imitation**, but mimesis imitates the *inner idea*
  embodied in a model, not its surface form. Students contemplate models/types (from
  literature, history, math, fine arts, nature) and are led inductively to grasp the idea.
- It is explicitly *not* ideology-pouring; it is a means of truth-perception.
- **Seven stages of a mimetic lesson** (student's perspective, with the teacher's
  five-stage counterparts in parentheses):
  1. **Gathering** — review prerequisite knowledge (teacher: Preparation)
  2. **Wondering** — the question the lesson will answer arises
  3. **Attending** — attend to the types/examples presented (Presentation)
  4. **Contemplating** — compare types; find what is essential (Comparison)
  5. **Defining** — the student articulates the idea or the steps of the skill (Expression)
  6. **Mastering** — practice by applying the idea (Application)
  7. **Resting** — confirmation from the teacher that the lesson is comprehended
- The teacher must first grasp the lesson's *logos* (core idea); examples, questions, and
  practice all serve that idea.

## Festina lente and unhurried mastery

*Festina lente* ("make haste slowly") is a recurring CIRCE pedagogical principle,
especially in its Master Teacher Apprenticeship: don't rush to techniques or chase trends;
dwell with great books, contemplate ideas, and take the time each step needs — mastery of
each step is the fastest way forward. It pairs with *multum non multa* ("much, not many"):
depth over coverage. (CIRCE apprenticeship blog; the principle pair also anchors Eben &
Perrin's *The Good Teacher*, sold by CIRCE.)

## Scheduling/product implications (Chiron)

- A CIRCE-flavored pack optimizes for **depth and contemplation, not coverage**: fewer
  works, longer dwell time, re-reading welcomed; "behind schedule" is a near-meaningless
  state (festina lente).
- Lesson templates should follow the **seven mimetic stages** — in particular, every block
  opens with Gathering (recall of prior knowledge) and closes with Resting (explicit
  confirmation), and the middle is contemplation of concrete models rather than
  explanation-first teaching.
- Daily interaction style: **Socratic/contemplative questions** about the idea in the
  model ("what is the same across these two examples?"), not recall quizzes.
- A "good day" is one in which the student genuinely contemplated something true, good,
  or beautiful and expressed the idea in their own words — an assessment closer to CM
  narration than to item scoring.
