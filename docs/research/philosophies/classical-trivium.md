# Classical Trivium (Well-Trained Mind lineage) — Research Notes (Chiron)

The modern homeschool "classical education" movement organizes K–12 around the trivium —
grammar, logic (dialectic), rhetoric — treated as developmental stages rather than merely
as the three medieval language arts. Two anchor sources: Dorothy Sayers' 1947 essay "The
Lost Tools of Learning" (the modern revival document) and Susan Wise Bauer & Jessie Wise's
*The Well-Trained Mind* (the dominant practical implementation, welltrainedmind.com).

## Sayers: "The Lost Tools of Learning" (1947)

Dorothy L. Sayers delivered the essay at Oxford in 1947. Her core argument: modern schools
teach "subjects" but never teach the art of learning itself; the medieval trivium taught
tools — grammar (facts and language), dialectic (logic and disputation), rhetoric
(expression) — which, once mastered, let a person learn anything. She famously remarked
that a student "remembers what he has learnt, but forgets altogether how he learned it."
(under 15 words, from the essay as hosted at gbt.org).

Sayers maps the trivium onto three developmental phases she observed in children:

| Sayers stage | Approx. phase | Character | Trivium art |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Poll-Parrot** | early childhood (roughly ages 4/6–11) | Memorizing is easy and enjoyable; reasoning is hard. Chants, rhymes, accumulating facts. | Grammar |
| **Pert** | early adolescence (roughly 12–14) | Argues, contradicts, catches others out; discursive reason awakens. | Dialectic / Logic |
| **Poetic** | mid-adolescence onward (roughly 14–16+) | Imaginative, self-expressive, seeks synthesis and begins to specialize. | Rhetoric |

Her practical implication: teach each mind what it is naturally hungry for at that stage —
memory work when memory is delightful, formal logic when the child is argumentative anyway,
elegant expression when the desire for self-expression peaks.

## The Well-Trained Mind (Bauer & Wise) framing

Susan Wise Bauer's welltrainedmind.com states the model directly: "Classical education
depends on a three-part process of training the mind" — note this is the document's single
direct quote; everything else here is paraphrase of the same page.

### Stage bands and emphasis

| Stage | Grades (ages) | Emphasis |
|---|---|---|
| **Grammar stage** | grades 1–4 (~6–10) | Absorbing facts and building blocks: phonics, spelling and grammar rules, poems, foreign-language vocabulary, stories of history and literature, math facts, descriptions of the natural world. Memorization is treated as fun at this age; the goal is a well-stocked mind, not self-expression. |
| **Logic stage** | grades 5–8 (~10–14) | Analytical thinking: the child asks "why," studies formal logic, begins algebra, and applies logic across subjects — thesis-driven writing, critical reading, cause-and-effect history, the scientific method. Triggered by the maturing capacity for abstract thought. |
| **Rhetoric stage** | grades 9–12 (~14–18) | Forceful, original expression: applies logic-stage tools to grammar-stage knowledge and expresses conclusions in clear, elegant language. Specialization begins — college courses, apprenticeships, travel, depth in a chosen field. |

### Distinctives beyond the stage model

- **Language-focused**: learning happens through words (reading, writing, speaking) rather
  than images/video; the WTM page argues language forces the mind to work where images let
  it stay passive.
- **History as the organizing spine**: twelve years = three repetitions of a four-year
  chronological cycle — Ancients → Middle Ages → Renaissance/Reformation → Modern — studied
  at grammar, logic, then rhetoric depth. Literature, art, and music readings track the
  history period being studied (Iliad picture-book in grade 1; the real text later).
- **Science in a matching four-year rotation**: biology/classification/human body → earth
  science and astronomy → chemistry → physics/computer science, roughly following the
  order of scientific discovery.
- **All knowledge interrelated**: subjects are deliberately linked (astronomy with history
  of science; the Odyssey with Greek history and the nature of heroism) rather than
  studied in isolation.

## Scheduling/product implications (Chiron)

- Stage assignment is by grade band with soft edges; a pack should key on the child's
  demonstrated phase (Sayers' behavioral markers: loves memorizing / loves arguing /
  seeks self-expression) with grade as the default.
- Grammar-stage blocks are memory-work-heavy (chants, songs, recitation, copyable facts);
  logic-stage blocks add formal logic and "why" discussion; rhetoric-stage blocks add
  essay/speech output and elective depth.
- The four-year history cycle is a first-class scheduling object: year-of-cycle determines
  literature, art, and music selections across the whole family (siblings study the same
  period at different depths — a deliberate WTM efficiency for homeschool parents).
