# 02 — Objection library: 20 objections, grouped by theme

Format: **Short** — say it, then stop talking. The silence is where they tell you the real objection. **Long** — only if they push.

Every answer traces to the claims spine or shipped code; nothing here overclaims. Standing rules: zero named customers said plainly; never confirm or deny the AI vendor (crib sheet governs); never say "nothing is stored" or "it forgets" (banned framings — the truthful line is the two buckets: raw tool data stays in their tools, the service's memory of their business persists for the account's life and is theirs).

---

## Trust (5)

**1. "You don't have any case studies."**

Short: > "Right — zero, and I won't dress that up. That's exactly why the offer is three months free and weekly access to the founder. You'd be first, on purpose, with terms priced for it."

Long: > "The design-partner program is the honest version of this moment: you get attention no later customer will ever get, and we get the on-record proof. What I can show you today instead of logos is the working product, live — and the fact that the same fleet pattern runs a real brokerage's operations daily. If you need referenceable customers before you move, that's a fair bar; can I come back when we have them?"

**2. "You're a solo founder."**

Short: > "I am, and the product is built around that fact rather than in spite of it — the service runs on schedule whether or not I'm at my desk. My job is the review cadence and the roadmap, not typing your drafts."

Long: > "The honest test isn't headcount, it's what breaks if I get hit by a bus. Answer: drafts stop arriving. Your email, your CRM, your books are untouched, because they were never in our hands — we read them where they live. And you're month-to-month with your data exportable, so the exit is a door, not a negotiation."

**3. "How do I know you'll be around in a year?"**

Short: > "You don't, and I won't pretend a roadmap slide changes that. What I can do is bound your downside: month-to-month, money-back guarantee, and your working records never leave your own systems."

Long: > "Look at what you'd actually lose if we vanished: drafts you hadn't approved yet, and the account's memory of your preferences — which you can export any time. Not your leads, not your books, not your client history. Those stay in your tools by architecture. The risk you're pricing is 'I have to stop using a service,' not 'my business is hostage to a startup.'"

**4. "What happens if you get acquired?"**

Short: > "Then whoever buys it inherits a month-to-month customer who can export their data and leave. That's the strongest guarantee I can honestly offer — I can't promise you the future, so I built the exit instead."

Long: > "There's no long contract to be trapped in and no data warehouse to be sold with — your raw records live in your systems, and the service memory is contractually yours, exportable and deletable. If a new owner made the service worse, you'd cancel that month. Design partners also get their case study approved word by word, so nothing about your business goes anywhere without your signature either way."

**5. "Who else has bought this?"**

Short: > "Nobody I can name yet — the first design partners are signing now, and I don't invent references. What I can do is show you the product live, today, instead."

Long: > "When partners are on record, you'll be able to call them — that's literally a term of the program: the testimonial only happens after they've genuinely seen value. Until then, the demo and the terms carry the argument. If you'd rather wait for the references, tell me and I'll check back when they exist."

## AI-specific (5) — vendor invisibility governs every one of these

**6. "What AI is this? Is it ChatGPT? Claude?"**

Short: > "We don't discuss which models we run — we manage that layer so you don't have to. What I can tell you exactly is what it does with your data, and where every draft waits for your approval."

Long: > "You're buying the service and the accountability, not a model brand — the same way you hired your accountant without asking which software they run. The AI layer underneath changes as capabilities evolve; the parts you're contracting for don't: your data handling is in our privacy policy, our subprocessors are listed there, and nothing goes out without your approval." *(Never confirm or deny a vendor name, in any direction, even casually. The subprocessor list on /privacy and /security is the sole sanctioned disclosure — full handling in `03-model-vendor-invisibility-crib-sheet.md`.)*

**7. "How is this different from ChatGPT?"**

Short: > "A chat window answers when you type at it — nothing happens when you don't. This reads your CRM, your inbox, your calendar on a schedule and shows up with the work already drafted, every day, without being asked."

Long: > "The difference is everything around the AI: connected to your actual systems, a compliance pass for your industry's rules, an approval queue, memory of your business that builds for the life of the account, and a person who installs and reviews it with you monthly. If typing at a chatbot were solving this, it would have solved it already — your Tuesday would look different." *(Compare with DIY-vs-run-for-you, never model-vs-model. We do not position against any AI vendor as a competitor.)*

**8. "What if the AI messes up?"**

Short: > "Then you reject a draft in five seconds instead of a client receiving a bad email. It can't send anything — every draft waits in your queue until you approve it. That gate is the product."

Long: > "There's no auto-send mode to misconfigure — there is no outbound path from our side, by architecture. A compliance check reviews drafts and flags issues before you even see them; it advises, you decide. And rejecting a draft with a reason teaches the system — 'doesn't sound like us' is a button. The worst case is a wasted draft, not a fair-housing complaint."

**9. "Does the AI see my client's data?"**

Short: > "It reads what it needs to draft — the lead's email, the calendar, the invoice — where that data already lives, in your systems. We don't copy your records into some warehouse of ours."

Long: > "Two buckets, plainly. Your raw tool data — the emails, the books, the CRM records — stays in your tools; the service reads it in flight to do the work. What we keep is the working memory of your business — your preferences, your voice, your corrections — because that's what makes it useful, and it's yours: exportable and deletable any time. The full detail is on our privacy and security pages, and I'd genuinely rather you read them than take my word."

**10. "Will you train on my data?"**

Short: > "Your data works for you, not for our other customers. The learning that happens is your account's own memory — your voice, your preferences — and it stays yours."

Long: > "What improves from your usage is *your* workspace: every edit and rejection teaches your service how you sound. We don't take your client records and use them to build the product for somebody else. The formal data-handling terms live in the privacy policy, and our subprocessors are listed on the security page — if your broker or attorney wants that in writing, send them both pages and I'll answer whatever's left." *(Do not name any vendor or make claims about a vendor's training practices — point to the published policy pages and offer written follow-up.)*

## Pricing (4)

**11. "How much is it?"**

Short: > "It's on the pricing page in public numbers — Regular is $199 a seat, sliding to $99. For you, as a design partner, the first three months are free. The real question is what the drafting work costs you now."

Long: > "The way we model it, the work this covers is worth $2,900 to $10,600 a month in owner time — and I'll flag honestly that that's a model, not a customer-attested result, because we don't have customers to attest yet. That's exactly why the pilot is free: run the model against your own week and let your Tuesday do the math."

**12. "That seems expensive for AI."**

Short: > "For AI, it would be — a chatbot subscription is twenty bucks. You're not buying AI; you're buying the service that runs it: installed, connected to your systems, compliance-checked, reviewed with you monthly, with a person accountable."

Long: > "Compare it to what actually competes with it. A good assistant is $3,000 to $4,500 a month, needs managing, and leaves with the training. Doing it yourself with a chatbot and some glue costs the thing you have least of — your evenings — and comes with no compliance pass and no one accountable at month-end. Against the work it replaces, it's the cheapest thing on the list. And for three months, it's free — so the price of testing that claim is zero."

**13. "Can I pay monthly?"**

Short: > "It's *only* monthly — there's no annual contract to sign and no lock-in to negotiate. Month-to-month is the whole billing model."

Long: > "That's deliberate. We're new, and asking you to trust us a year at a time before we've earned a quarter would be backwards. The subscription earns its renewal every month or you stop paying — that's the accountability structure, not a discount lever."

**14. "What if I want to cancel?"**

Short: > "You cancel, that month, from the account page — and your data exports with you. No retention call, no notice period, no hostage negotiation."

Long: > "The pattern to notice: your raw business records were never in our hands to begin with — your CRM, your inbox, your books stay yours by architecture. What you'd export is the service's memory of your business — preferences, history — and that's yours too. On a paid plan there's also a money-back guarantee window, on the pricing page in plain terms. The exit door being real is a feature we sell, not a risk we hide."

## Fit (3)

**15. "We already use Follow Up Boss and it works fine."**

Short: > "Keep it — seriously. Follow Up Boss is your system of record, and we connect to it rather than compete with it. The question is who writes the reply to the lead FUB logged at 9pm."

Long: > "FUB is great at organizing leads; it doesn't draft the work between the records. That's the seam we live in: the after-hours lead lands in FUB, and by morning there's a drafted reply, two showing windows, and the CRM update sitting in your approval queue — connected to your FUB with an API key, live today, not roadmap. If your leads aren't organized, buy FUB first. If they're organized and still waiting until 9am for a human, that's us."

**16. "We tried a chatbot and it didn't stick."**

Short: > "What usually dies is a tool somebody has to remember to open. This shows up in the inbox and the queue you already check — the habit is 'review,' not 'operate.'"

Long: > "What made the last one not stick? *(Listen. Log it verbatim.)* Most attempts fail on exactly the parts we took off your plate: someone had to prompt it, connect it, and babysit it. Here the setup is on us, the work arrives drafted on a schedule, and the monthly review exists precisely to catch 'nobody's opening the queue' before it becomes 'we quit.' If it doesn't stick in your shop, we'd both see it in the numbers by week two — for free."

**17. "My team won't adopt this."**

Short: > "They don't have to adopt much — nothing is ripped out and nothing new has to be learned. Their tools stay; the drafts arrive where they already work; the one new habit is opening a queue with coffee."

Long: > "In the pilot we deliberately keep it to one named operator — usually you — so there's no team rollout to survive. Ten minutes a morning: approve, edit, or toss. When it's earning its keep, your admin becomes the reviewer, which makes that job more senior, not obsolete — they stop typing the follow-ups and start signing them. If the team hates it at week four, that's exactly what the weekly call is for, and the exit is a month away at most."

## Legal / compliance (3)

**18. "What about license-law and MLS compliance?" (GREC/TREC territory)**

Short: > "The compliance answer is the architecture: nothing reaches a client without a licensee approving it, so the sender of record is always you, not an algorithm. We never auto-post anywhere — not to clients, not to the MLS."

Long: > "Two layers. First, drafts get a compliance review pass — for real estate that includes fair-housing screening, live today — which flags issues before you even see the draft. Second, and more importantly, the human gate: an auto-send tool has to convince your regulator its algorithm is compliant; we never have to, because a licensed human approves every send. I won't claim a certification we don't hold — what I'll claim is that the liable person keeps the pen, which is the posture your E&O carrier and your commission both actually want."

**19. "Where does the data live? We have data-locality requirements."**

Short: > "Your business records live where they live today — in your CRM, your inbox, your books; we read them there rather than relocating them. The service's own memory runs on the infrastructure providers listed publicly on our security page."

Long: > "If you have a hard residency requirement — contractual or regulatory — tell me exactly what it says and I'll answer it in writing against our subprocessor list rather than hand-wave it on a call. I'd rather lose a week being precise than win a call being vague. The honest headline stands either way: the sensitive mass of your data never migrates to us in the first place."

**20. "What's your DPA?"**

Short: > "We don't have a signature-ready DPA today, and I won't improvise one on a call. What's published now: the privacy policy and the security page, including the subprocessor list."

Long: > "If a signed DPA is a hard gate for you, we're honestly early for your procurement process — I'd rather say that now than at contract time. If it's a comfort question, the published pages plus the architecture — your records stay in your systems, nothing sends without you — usually answer the real concern. Either way I log every DPA ask, because the count of those asks is exactly what pulls it up our legal queue." *(Log it. The legal plan's counsel packet is gated on demand signal — this is the demand signal.)*
