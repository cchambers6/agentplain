# 03 — Model-vendor invisibility crib sheet

**The rule (load-bearing, ratified):** never confirm or deny which AI model or vendor runs underneath — in any direction, in any phrasing, even casually, even by omission-that-implies ("I can't say, but you can guess"). No vendor or model name crosses Conner's lips on a customer call. The **sole sanctioned disclosure** is the subprocessor list on /privacy and /security — it exists for legal transparency, and it speaks for itself; Conner does not narrate it.

**Why (so the discipline holds under pressure):** the value and the moat are the service layer — the skills, the memory, the approval architecture, the accountability. Naming the model commoditizes us against it ("why not just use that directly?"), and it ages badly as the layer underneath evolves. The redirect is always to **our service partnership**, never to "the model we use."

**Delivery note:** these arrive without warning, usually mid-demo. Hesitation reads as hiding something; a smooth, warm, complete answer reads as professionalism. Memorize variant 3 — the others are inflections of it.

---

## The five variants, ranked by directness of the prospect's question

### 1. Casual curiosity — "So what's this built on, under the hood?"

> "The honest answer is that the under-the-hood layer is our job, not your problem — we select it, run it, and swap it as capabilities evolve, the same way your accountant picks their own software. What I'd rather show you is the part you'll actually live with: where the drafts come from and where they wait for your approval."

*Then immediately return to the demo. Curiosity at this level is satisfied by confidence, not information.*

### 2. Comparison framing — "Is this basically ChatGPT behind the scenes?"

> "It's a different kind of thing, so 'behind the scenes' is the wrong layer to compare on. A chat window waits for you to type; this reads your systems on a schedule and shows up with the work drafted. Which AI runs underneath isn't something we discuss — what makes it work for a brokerage is everything wrapped around it: the connections, the compliance pass, the approval queue, and us running it."

*Note the move: decline the vendor comparison and replace it with the DIY-vs-run-for-you comparison, which is ours to win.*

### 3. Direct vendor question — "Which model is it — GPT? Claude? Something else?" *(memorize this one)*

> "We don't discuss which models we run — we manage that layer so you don't have to, and it changes as capabilities evolve. What I can tell you exactly, and in writing: what the service does with your data, who our subprocessors are — that's published on our security page — and the fact that nothing goes out without your approval. Those are the parts you're actually contracting for."

### 4. Pressed — "I'd really need to know which AI vendor before I could sign anything."

> "Fair, and here's the distinction that I think answers it: our craft is designing and running the service on top — I don't get into the underlying model choice on calls because it genuinely changes as capabilities evolve, and I don't want to tell you something today that's stale in a quarter. What doesn't change is what you'd be signing: the privacy policy, the subprocessor list on the security page — which is the formal, legal answer to your question — and the approval architecture. If your review process needs more than those pages give you, tell me what it asks for and I'll respond in writing."

*The subprocessor list IS the answer for a diligence process — route them to it; never paraphrase or summarize its contents aloud.*

### 5. Technical or security buyer — "Our IT consultant will ask which LLM provider you use, what the API terms are, whether prompts are retained."

> "Good — that's the right person to be asking, and I'll give them a better artifact than my word on a sales call: our security page and privacy policy, which carry the subprocessor list and the data-handling terms. Send me their questions and they'll get written answers against those documents. The two things I'll say with confidence right now: your business records stay in your systems — we read them where they live, we don't warehouse them — and there is no path where AI output reaches a client without a human at your firm approving it."

*Written follow-up against published documents, always. Never freelance vendor terms, retention claims, or API details on a live call.*

---

## Hard rails

- **Never confirm, never deny, never wink.** "I can neither confirm nor deny" is also banned — it's a spy-movie tell that invites digging. The posture is "that layer is our job," not "that's a secret."
- **Never disparage or praise any AI vendor by name.** We are not a competitor to any model and never frame one as our rival — the comparison set is tools and labor (DIY, an assistant, an agency), never models.
- **Never let a prospect's guess stand as confirmation.** If they say "so it's definitely GPT then," the response is variant 3 again, warmly: "Same answer as before — we don't discuss the model layer either way. Here's what I *can* nail down for you…"
- **The subprocessor list is pointed at, not performed.** Say "the security page lists our subprocessors" — do not recite names from it on the call. It exists as a legal document; let it do its legal job.
- **If they make it a dealbreaker:** "If knowing the model brand is a hard requirement, we may honestly be the wrong shape of vendor for you — we sell the service and the accountability, and the model layer stays ours to manage. I'd rather tell you that than pretend otherwise." Log the ask in the living sections; the count matters.
