# 04 — Disqualification framework: when to walk away on the call

A "no" with a stated reason is a deliverable, not a failure — and a bad-fit yes is worse than any no: it burns the weekly-call capacity (five design-partner slots, one founder), produces a case study we can't use, and converts as churn. Disqualify **warmly and specifically, on the call** — never by ghosting afterward.

Every disqualification still ends with the learning question — *"what would have had to be true for this to be a yes?"* — and a verbatim log entry in the parent playbook's living sections.

This extends the parent's §4 disqualifiers (locked franchise stack, PMS-only books, SOC 2 procurement gate, wants auto-send, regulated business through the general door, no nameable recurring pain). Below are the five signals most likely on this cohort's calls, with the polite-close words.

---

## Signal 1 — Brokerage below ~5 agents (too small)

**How it shows up:** Question 2 surfaces it — "just me and one agent," no staff touching email or books. The after-hours volume and the drafting load usually can't cover the subscription's value story, and the case study won't generalize.

**Judgment call, not a hard rule:** a tiny shop with brutal named pain (a solo broker drowning in a lead flood) can still fit. But if Question 1 produced a mild Tuesday *and* the shop is under five agents, close it out.

**The polite close:**

> "Here's my honest read, and it's about timing, not you: at your size, the drafting load we take over probably doesn't pay for itself yet — and I'd rather tell you that than sell you a service that underwhelms. The moment that changes is when you're adding agents and the follow-up starts slipping. Can I check back when you're staffing up — and would you want the one-pager in the meantime so you know what to reach for when it happens?"

## Signal 2 — Doesn't own their tech-stack decisions

**How it shows up:** "Corporate handles our tools," "everything runs through the franchise's platform," "I'd have to ask the national office before connecting anything." If they can't authorize an API key or an email connection, there is no activation path — this month or any month.

**The polite close:**

> "That changes the picture, and I want to be straight about why: everything we do starts with connecting to your CRM and your inbox, and if those decisions live at the franchise level, I'd be selling you a service you're not allowed to switch on. If you think the franchise would entertain it, I'm glad to talk to whoever owns that — otherwise the honest move is for me not to take more of your time. Who would that conversation belong to, if anyone?"

*(If they name a real owner, that's a referral, not a loss — log it as a new prospect row.)*

## Signal 3 — Wants a discount before seeing the demo

**How it shows up:** price pressure at minute 3 — "before we go further, what kind of deal can you do?" — before any pain is named or any product is seen. On a design-partner motion this is doubly disqualifying: the pilot is *already free*, so a discount ask before the demo signals shopping for cheap, not shopping for the outcome.

**The polite close:**

> "I'll make the money part easy, because it's not where this decision lives: the design-partner pilot is three months free — there's nothing to discount. The real cost is the twenty minutes we're in and a weekly call after — and if the service doesn't earn its price after three free months of evidence, you shouldn't pay it, full stop. So the useful question isn't the deal, it's whether your week has the work in it. Can I ask you about last Tuesday?"

*(One redirect, then judge: if the next sentence is another price maneuver, close warmly — "I don't think we're the vendor you're shopping for, and that's okay. If a free pilot ever sounds like the interesting part, you know where I am.")*

## Signal 4 — Needs approval from someone not on the call

**How it shows up:** "I'd have to run this by my partner / my broker / my spouse-co-owner." Not fatal by itself — but authority absent *and unreachable* means anything agreed today isn't real, and "yes-pending-partner" rows rot in the CRM.

**The move: don't close the person in front of you — book the person who can say yes.**

> "That's completely reasonable, and let's not do the thing where you try to re-explain a demo secondhand — it never survives the retelling. Two options, your pick: we book twenty minutes with both of you and I run this exact call again — I genuinely don't mind — or I send you the recorded walkthrough and the one-pager tonight and you two watch it together. Either way, when should the three of us know one way or the other?"

*(A calendar entry with the decision-maker is a live row. "I'll show them the video sometime" gets a named check-back date or it's logged as not-yet.)*

## Signal 5 — "We should compare with X first" as a stall

**How it shows up:** late in the call, usually after the price — "we'd want to look at what BoldTrail's AI does first," "our CRM says they're launching something like this." Distinguish honestly: a *real* evaluation criterion deserves a real answer (objection #15 — keep your CRM; we work the seams it doesn't). A *stall* is when "compare" has no named tool, no named criterion, and no date.

**First, one honest test:**

> "That's fair — what specifically would you want to see X do before this made sense? I ask because if their built-in assistant covers it, you should use it and keep your money."

**If a real criterion comes back:** answer it from the objection library, honestly, including where the incumbent wins first. **If nothing specific comes back, it's a stall — close it warmly with a date:**

> "Tell you what — I don't want to be the vendor who talks you out of due diligence. Go look. The design-partner slots are five total, and they fill in the order people commit, so here's what I'll do: I'll check back on [specific date, ≤2 weeks out]. If the comparison turned up something we should beat, tell me what it was — that's genuinely useful to me either way."

*(Scarcity line is only usable while true: five slots is the ratified program size. Never inflate urgency beyond the real number.)*

---

## What every walk-away has in common

1. **The reason is said out loud, specifically** — "too small yet," "franchise owns the stack" — never a vague fade.
2. **A revisit condition is named when one honestly exists** — and goes in the CRM with a date.
3. **The learning question gets asked** — verbatim answer logged. At a five-prospect cohort size, that answer is worth more than the call.
4. **The door stays open and warm.** Georgia RE is a small community; every disqualified broker is a future referral source who should hang up thinking "straight shooter," not "salesman."
