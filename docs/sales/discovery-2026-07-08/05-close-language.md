# 05 — Close language: minute 18–20

The rule that governs everything here: **the call never ends on "we'll follow up" or "I'll circle back."** A yes is a calendar entry made before the goodbye. A warm-maybe is a named blocker and a named date in the CRM. A no gets the learning question. Those are the only three exits; "we'll be in touch" is not one of them.

The transition line into the close, at minute 18 regardless of where the conversation is:

> "We're coming up on the twenty minutes I asked you for, so let me land this respectfully. Based on what you've told me about [their Tuesday / their named pain], here's where I honestly think we are…"

*(Then say which of the two paths you're on. Naming it out loud — "I think this is a fit" or "I think this is a not-yet" — is the move; prospects respect a vendor who scores the call in front of them.)*

---

## Path A — Yes: book the kickoff before the goodbye

**Step 1 — call the fit, restate the deal in one breath:**

> "I think you're exactly who this program is for. So to say the deal back in one sentence: three months free, a weekly 30 minutes with me, you go on record once you've genuinely seen value, and we write the case study together — you approve every word before anyone sees it. Still sound right?"

**Step 2 — the booking ask (the actual close):**

> "Then let's book the kickoff right now while we've both got calendars open — it's 90 minutes on screen-share, and I'll make you one promise about it: **before we hang up that call, you'll have approved a real item, with your own hands, in your own workspace.** Not a demo — your leads, your inbox. What does [day within ~1 week] look like?"

**Step 3 — set up the one thing they do before kickoff:**

> "Between now and then, exactly one homework item, five minutes: I'll send you an email today with a couple of setup steps — the main one is grabbing an API key from your Follow Up Boss account, and the email walks you through it click by click. Everything else happens on the call, with me driving the boring parts and you driving everything that matters."

**Step 4 — last words:**

> "You'll have three things from me by end of day: the calendar invite for the kickoff, the recurring invite for our weekly Friday call — accept both — and a short letter about the case study we'll build together, so the on-record part never surprises you. See you [day]."

**Conner's same-day checklist (all four, or the yes isn't landed):**
- ☐ Kickoff invite sent (90 min) — the Day-1 activation call, run per `docs/pilot/week-1-runbook/01-DAY-1-activation-call.md`
- ☐ Recurring Friday-call series invite sent
- ☐ Day-0 prep email sent (FUB API key walkthrough + what to expect), per `docs/pilot/week-1-runbook/00-DAY-0-partner-signed.md`
- ☐ Case-study-carrot letter sent within 24h (`docs/outreach/2026-07-03-design-partner-kit/05-case-study-carrot.md`) + CRM row → QUALIFIED/kickoff-booked

**If they say yes-but-not-the-calendar ("just email me some times"):** treat it as a warm-maybe wearing a yes costume. One push, warmly: > "Happy to — and I've learned 'email me times' is where good intentions go to die, so let's just pick one now and you can move it later, free of charge." If they still won't book, Path B.

---

## Path B — Warm maybe: date it or it didn't happen

A warm-maybe is a **good outcome when it's true** — a named blocker with a named revisit date beats a soft yes that no-shows the kickoff. The close has to produce both the blocker and the date, out loud.

**Step 1 — name the state, take the pressure off:**

> "It sounds like this is interesting but not a today-decision, and that's a completely fine answer — I'd rather have a real 'not yet' than a polite yes that ghosts me. Two questions so I don't become a pest:"

**Step 2 — the two questions (both get answered before goodbye):**

> "One — what would need to be true for this to be worth doing? Not for my pitch, for real: budget cycle, the busy season ending, your partner seeing it, a tool we don't connect yet?"

> "Two — when should I check back? Give me a real date and I promise two things: I *will* check in on that date, and I *won't* before it."

**Step 3 — the follow-up email trigger, stated on the call:**

> "Here's what I'll send you today so this doesn't rot in both our inboxes: the one-pager, and the recorded walkthrough of what I showed you — that one's especially useful if [the absent partner / the skeptical team] should see it without another meeting. Then you won't hear from me until [their date], unless you reach out first."

**Conner's same-day checklist:**
- ☐ CRM row → NOT-YET, with the blocker verbatim and the revisit date as the next action
- ☐ Follow-up email sent same day: one-pager + recorded walkthrough + one sentence restating their blocker and their date in their own words ("You said the moment to look again is after closing season — talk to you the week of the 15th.")
- ☐ Sequence choice honored: a *stated* not-now gets the stated date, not the drip; only silence after this email falls into the standard T+3/7/14/21 chain (`docs/outreach/2026-07-03-design-partner-kit/03-reply-follow-up-chain.md`)
- ☐ One line into the living sections: what the blocker was — the blocker census is roadmap input

**The kept-promise rule:** check back on the date, on the day, referencing their words. The entire trust position of this company with zero logos is built on doing exactly what we said, at the small scale, repeatedly. A follow-up that arrives when promised *is* the product demo.

---

## Path C — No (for completeness; full handling in the parent playbook)

> "I appreciate the straight answer — genuinely. One question for my own learning, then I'll let you go: what would have had to be true for this to be a yes?"

Log the answer verbatim. CRM row → LOST with the reason. Thank them, mean it, hang up warm. Never argue with a no, never sneak a no into the drip sequence — a no is a no.
